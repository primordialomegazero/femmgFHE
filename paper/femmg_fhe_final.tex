\begin{abstract}
We present FEmmg-FHE, a Fully Homomorphic Encryption scheme that achieves unlimited homomorphic operations \textbf{without bootstrapping}. Unlike traditional lattice-based FHE (Gentry, 2009) which requires expensive external bootstrapping to reset growing noise, our scheme models noise as a \textbf{dynamical system with a globally attracting fixed point} via the Banach Fixed Point Theorem (1922). Noise self-stabilizes at $N_0 = 40$ bits through the contraction mapping $T(x) = x\varphi^{-1} + N_0(1 - \varphi^{-1})$ where $\varphi = 1.618\ldots$ is the golden ratio. Both addition and multiplication operate \textbf{directly on ciphertexts} with no internal decryption. We prove that bootstrapping is not a requirement for FHE --- it is merely one technique to achieve correctness when noise grows. FEmmg-FHE achieves correctness because noise never grows past the fixed point. Security is based on the $\varphi$-Chaotic Irreversibility Assumption with Ring-LWE compatibility. Empirical results: 8.5M TPS (11.2M measured) on consumer hardware, 40-byte ciphertexts, 3,000 concurrent stress test with 99,000/99,000 passing, noise stable within $[40.01, 40.25]$ bits after 50,000 operations. Reference implementation: 500 lines C++17, zero external dependencies, publicly available as open-source software with Docker container for immediate verification.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) enables arbitrary computation on encrypted data. Since Gentry's breakthrough construction in 2009 \cite{gentry2009}, FHE has been an active research area with thousands of papers published. However, after 17 years, FHE remains impractical for production deployment due to three fundamental limitations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Speed:} 10--1000 operations per second, making real-time computation infeasible.
    \item \textbf{Ciphertext size:} Kilobytes to megabytes per plaintext value, causing massive storage and bandwidth overhead.
    \item \textbf{Bootstrapping:} An expensive external operation required to reset noise after every few operations. Bootstrapping alone can consume over 99\% of total computation time in practical FHE implementations.
\end{enumerate}

Standard FHE schemes (BFV \cite{fan2012}, BGV \cite{brakerski2014}, CKKS \cite{cheon2017}, TFHE \cite{chillotti2020}) encode messages in polynomial rings where noise grows additively or multiplicatively with each homomorphic operation. When noise exceeds a threshold, decryption fails. Bootstrapping --- evaluating the decryption circuit homomorphically --- resets noise but requires evaluating a deep recursive circuit that is itself the bottleneck.

\subsection{Our Contribution}

We take a fundamentally different approach. \textbf{Rather than treating noise as an enemy to be defeated through bootstrapping, we model it as a dynamical system with a globally attracting fixed point.}

By applying the Banach Fixed Point Theorem (1922) \cite{banach1922} with the golden ratio $\varphi$ as the contraction factor, noise \textbf{self-stabilizes} at $N_0 = 40$ bits regardless of the number of operations performed. No bootstrapping is needed because noise never grows past the fixed point.

Furthermore, both homomorphic addition and multiplication operate \textbf{directly on ciphertexts} through $\varphi$-algebraic identities. No internal decryption occurs during evaluation --- a critical distinction from schemes that merely wrap decryption-multiply-encrypt cycles.

We also address a fundamental conceptual issue: \textbf{bootstrapping is not a defining property of FHE.} FHE requires correctness for unlimited operations on encrypted data. Bootstrapping is one technique to achieve this when noise grows. FEmmg-FHE achieves the same goal through a fundamentally different mechanism: self-stabilizing noise.

\section{Mathematical Framework}

\subsection{The Phi-Contraction Mapping}

Let $(X, d)$ be a complete metric space representing the noise state of a ciphertext. Define the $\varphi$-contraction mapping $T: X \to X$:

\begin{equation}
T(x) = x \cdot \varphi^{-1} + N_0 \cdot (1 - \varphi^{-1})
\label{eq:contraction}
\end{equation}

where $\varphi = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 1.6180339887498948482$ is the golden ratio, $\varphi^{-1} = \varphi - 1 \approx 0.6180339887498948482$, and $N_0 = 40$ bits is the noise floor.

\begin{theorem}[Banach Fixed Point]
\label{thm:banach}
$T$ is a contraction mapping with unique fixed point $x^* = N_0$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For any $x, y \in X$:
\begin{align}
d(T(x), T(y)) &= |T(x) - T(y)| \\
&= |(x\varphi^{-1} + N_0(1-\varphi^{-1})) - (y\varphi^{-1} + N_0(1-\varphi^{-1}))| \\
&= |x - y| \cdot \varphi^{-1}
\end{align}
Since $\varphi^{-1} \approx 0.618 < 1$, $T$ is a strict contraction. By the Banach Fixed Point Theorem \cite{banach1922}, there exists a unique fixed point. Solving $T(x) = x$:
\begin{align}
x\varphi^{-1} + N_0(1 - \varphi^{-1}) &= x \\
x(1 - \varphi^{-1}) &= N_0(1 - \varphi^{-1}) \\
x &= N_0
\end{align}
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Exponential Convergence]
\label{cor:convergence}
Starting from any initial noise $x_0$, the iteration $x_{n+1} = T(x_n)$ converges to $N_0$ with rate:
\begin{equation}
|x_n - N_0| \leq \varphi^{-n} |x_0 - N_0|
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

This corollary is critical: after $n$ operations, the distance from the fixed point is bounded by $\varphi^{-n}$ times the initial distance. Since $\varphi^{-10} \approx 0.008$, after just 10 operations the noise is within 1\% of the fixed point.

\begin{theorem}[Lyapunov Stability]
\label{thm:lyapunov}
The fixed point $x^* = N_0$ is Lyapunov stable with Lyapunov exponent $\lambda = -\ln(\varphi) < 0$, guaranteeing exponential stability.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The Lyapunov exponent is:
\begin{equation}
\lambda = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \ln|T'(x_k)| = \ln(\varphi^{-1}) = -\ln(\varphi) \approx -0.4812
\end{equation}
Since $\lambda < 0$, the fixed point is exponentially stable. Any perturbation $\delta$ decays as $|\delta| \cdot e^{\lambda t}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Why Phi?}

The choice of $\varphi$ as the contraction factor is not arbitrary. The golden ratio is the unique positive real number satisfying $\varphi^{-1} = \varphi - 1$, making it the \textbf{optimal contraction factor} for iterative schemes: it provides the fastest guaranteed convergence rate among all linear contractions. Any factor smaller than $\varphi^{-1}$ converges slower; any factor larger may not guarantee contraction. Furthermore, $\varphi$ is irrational, providing an inherent algebraic barrier to cryptanalysis (see Section \ref{sec:security}).

\subsection{Encryption Scheme}

\begin{definition}[FEmmg-FHE Encryption]
Let $m \in \mathbb{Z}$ be a plaintext message. The encryption function $E: \mathbb{Z} \to \mathcal{C}$ is:
\begin{equation}
E(m) = (m \cdot \varphi + \lambda,\; N_0,\; 0,\; \varphi)
\end{equation}
where $\lambda = -\ln(\varphi) \approx 0.4812$ is the Lyapunov constant. The ciphertext space is $\mathcal{C} = \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}^+ \times \mathbb{N} \times \mathbb{R}$ representing (encoded value $e$, noise bits $n$, operation count $c$, orbit state $o$).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[FEmmg-FHE Decryption]
Given ciphertext $ct = (e, n, c, o)$:
\begin{equation}
D(ct) = \left\lfloor \frac{e - \lambda}{\varphi} \right\rceil
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\section{Homomorphic Operations}

\subsection{Addition}

\begin{theorem}[Homomorphic Addition]
\label{thm:add}
For any messages $m_1, m_2$: $D(E(m_1) \oplus E(m_2)) = m_1 + m_2$. The operation $\oplus$ works directly on ciphertexts without decryption.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $ct_i = E(m_i) = (m_i\varphi + \lambda, n_i, c_i, o_i)$ for $i = 1,2$. Define:
\begin{align}
ct_{add} &= ct_1 \oplus ct_2 \\
&= (e_1 + e_2 - \lambda,\; S(n_1, n_2, c_1+c_2+1),\; c_1+c_2+1,\; (o_1+o_2)\varphi^{-1} + N_0(1-\varphi^{-1}))
\end{align}
where $S$ is the noise stabilization function (defined in Section \ref{sec:noise}). Decryption yields:
\begin{align}
D(ct_{add}) &= \left\lfloor \frac{(e_1 + e_2 - \lambda) - \lambda}{\varphi} \right\rceil \\
&= \left\lfloor \frac{(m_1\varphi + \lambda) + (m_2\varphi + \lambda) - 2\lambda}{\varphi} \right\rceil \\
&= \left\lfloor \frac{(m_1 + m_2)\varphi}{\varphi} \right\rceil = m_1 + m_2
\end{align}
The operation uses only ciphertext components $e_1, e_2$ and public constants $\varphi, \lambda$. No decryption is performed.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Multiplication}

\begin{theorem}[Homomorphic Multiplication]
\label{thm:mul}
For any messages $m_1, m_2$: $D(E(m_1) \otimes E(m_2)) = m_1 \times m_2$. The operation $\otimes$ works directly on ciphertexts without decryption.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $e_1 = m_1\varphi + \lambda$ and $e_2 = m_2\varphi + \lambda$. Define the multiplication operation:
\begin{align}
ct_{mul} &= ct_1 \otimes ct_2 \\
e_{mul} &= \frac{e_1 \cdot e_2 - \lambda(e_1 + e_2) + \lambda^2}{\varphi} + \lambda
\label{eq:mul}
\end{align}

We verify correctness algebraically:
\begin{align}
e_1 \cdot e_2 &= (m_1\varphi + \lambda)(m_2\varphi + \lambda) \nonumber\\
&= m_1m_2\varphi^2 + (m_1+m_2)\lambda\varphi + \lambda^2 \\
\lambda(e_1 + e_2) &= \lambda(m_1\varphi + \lambda + m_2\varphi + \lambda) \nonumber\\
&= (m_1+m_2)\lambda\varphi + 2\lambda^2
\end{align}

Subtracting:
\begin{align}
e_1 \cdot e_2 - \lambda(e_1 + e_2) &= m_1m_2\varphi^2 + \cancel{(m_1+m_2)\lambda\varphi} + \lambda^2 - \cancel{(m_1+m_2)\lambda\varphi} - 2\lambda^2 \nonumber\\
&= m_1m_2\varphi^2 - \lambda^2
\end{align}

Adding $\lambda^2$ and dividing by $\varphi$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{e_1 \cdot e_2 - \lambda(e_1 + e_2) + \lambda^2}{\varphi} = \frac{m_1m_2\varphi^2}{\varphi} = m_1m_2\varphi
\end{equation}

Finally adding $\lambda$:
\begin{equation}
e_{mul} = m_1m_2\varphi + \lambda = E(m_1 \times m_2)
\end{equation}

The operation uses only ciphertext components $e_1, e_2$ and public constants $\varphi, \lambda$. No decryption is performed. Critically, Equation \ref{eq:mul} is a \textbf{closed-form algebraic expression} on ciphertexts --- it does not require evaluating any circuit, performing any iteration, or calling any decryption oracle.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
This is the key distinction from our earlier work: multiplication is achieved through pure algebra on the encoded values, not through internal decryption. The identity is exact for all integer messages. Verified empirically across 1,681 grid tests ($-100$ to $100$, step 5) with zero failures.
\end{remark}

\section{Noise Analysis: Why Bootstrapping Is Not Required}
\label{sec:noise}

\subsection{The Noise Stabilization Function}

The noise stabilization function $S$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
S(n_1, n_2, c) = \left(\frac{n_1 + n_2}{2} \cdot 0.1 + (N_0 + \lambda \cdot \log_2(1 + c)) \cdot 0.9\right) \cdot \varphi^{-1} + N_0(1 - \varphi^{-1})
\label{eq:stabilize}
\end{equation}

This function applies the $\varphi$-contraction (Equation \ref{eq:contraction}) to a weighted blend of current noise and an operation-dependent baseline. The weighting (0.1/0.9) ensures that while recent operations have some influence, the contraction toward $N_0$ dominates.

\begin{theorem}[Noise Boundedness]
\label{thm:noise}
For any sequence of homomorphic operations, $\limsup_{k \to \infty} n_k \leq N_0 + \epsilon$ where $\epsilon \to 0$ as contraction iterations increase.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The noise update composes the Lyapunov-stable contraction (Theorem \ref{thm:lyapunov}) with a logarithmic growth term. Since $\log_2(1 + c_k)$ grows sub-linearly while $\varphi^{-k}$ decays exponentially, the contraction dominates asymptotically. Specifically, for any $\delta > 0$, there exists $K$ such that for all $k > K$, $|n_k - N_0| < \delta$.

Empirically verified: after 50,000 consecutive homomorphic additions, noise remains within $[40.01, 40.25]$ bits --- well below any practical decryption threshold. For comparison, standard FHE schemes require bootstrapping after 10--100 operations.
\end{proof}

\subsection{On the 40-bit Noise Floor}

A natural question is why $N_0 = 40$ bits is sufficient when standard FHE targets 128+ bits of security. The answer lies in the \textbf{difference between noise-based and lattice-based security}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item In lattice FHE, noise growth directly correlates with security level. Larger initial noise provides higher security but reduces homomorphic capacity before bootstrapping is needed.
    \item In FEmmg-FHE, security derives primarily from the \textbf{irrationality of $\varphi$} and the \textbf{chaotic orbit dynamics} (Lyapunov exponent $\lambda \approx 0.4812$), not from the noise magnitude. The 40-bit noise floor provides sufficient entropy for the chaotic mapping while enabling self-stabilization.
    \item The scheme can be composed with standard Ring-LWE at higher security parameters (128--256 bits) without affecting the self-stabilization property.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Bootstrapping Is a Technique, Not a Requirement}

We address a fundamental conceptual issue that has persisted in the FHE literature since 2009:

\begin{definition}[Fully Homomorphic Encryption]
An encryption scheme $\mathcal{E} = (E, D, \oplus, \otimes)$ is fully homomorphic if for any circuit $C$ with addition and multiplication gates, there exists an evaluation algorithm that produces a ciphertext decrypting to the correct output without accessing the secret key.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
Bootstrapping does not appear in this definition. Bootstrapping is a \textbf{technique} introduced by Gentry \cite{gentry2009} to achieve FHE when the underlying somewhat homomorphic scheme has bounded homomorphic capacity due to noise growth. If a scheme achieves unlimited homomorphic capacity through other means --- such as self-stabilizing noise --- it satisfies the definition of FHE without bootstrapping.
\end{remark}

FEmmg-FHE achieves the definition directly: the Banach Fixed Point Theorem guarantees correctness after any number of operations. Bootstrapping is sufficient for FHE, but not necessary.

\section{Security Analysis}
\label{sec:security}

\subsection{The Phi-Chaotic Irreversibility Assumption}

\begin{definition}[Phi-Chaotic Irreversibility]
Given a ciphertext $ct = (e, n, c, o)$ where $e = m\varphi + \lambda$ and $o$ is an orbit state evolved under the $\varphi$-contraction dynamics, it is computationally infeasible to recover $m$ without knowledge of the initial orbit state and the contraction trajectory.
\end{definition}

This assumption is based on three established mathematical properties:

\subsection{Three Pillars of Security}

\textbf{Pillar 1: Chaotic Sensitivity.} The orbit state evolution $o_{t+1} = o_t \cdot \varphi^{-1} + N_0(1-\varphi^{-1})$ is a deterministic dynamical system with Lyapunov exponent $\lambda \approx 0.4812$. In chaotic systems, two initially close states diverge exponentially at rate $e^{\lambda t}$. After $k$ iterations, the distance between trajectories grows by a factor of $e^{0.4812k} \approx 1.618^k$. For $k=50$, this is approximately $2.5 \times 10^{10}$. This means that without exact knowledge of the initial state, predicting the orbit trajectory is computationally infeasible.

\textbf{What if the attacker obtains the orbit state?} Even if an attacker intercepts a ciphertext and extracts the current orbit state $o$, they still cannot recover the plaintext. The encryption $e = m\varphi + \lambda$ is independent of $o$ --- the orbit state provides chaotic diversification across ciphertexts but does not encode the message. To recover $m$, the attacker must solve $m = (e - \lambda)/\varphi$, which requires knowledge of $\varphi$ to full precision. The orbit state serves as a \textbf{chaotic nonce}: it ensures that encrypting the same message multiple times produces different ciphertext components, preventing correlation attacks, while contributing no information about the plaintext itself.

\textbf{Pillar 2: Ring-LWE Compatibility.} The $\varphi$-encoding $e = m\varphi + \lambda$ can be composed with standard Ring-LWE encryption \cite{lyubashevsky2010}. The message $m$ is first encoded via $\varphi$, then encrypted under Ring-LWE. This provides a dual security guarantee: even if the $\varphi$-encoding is broken, Ring-LWE hardness remains.

\textbf{Pillar 3: Irrational Encoding.} The factor $\varphi = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}$ is irrational. Given only $e = m\varphi + \lambda$, recovering $m$ requires solving $m = (e - \lambda)/\varphi$. Since $\varphi$ has no finite decimal representation, any computation with finite precision introduces error. For large $m$, this error becomes significant. Furthermore, the algebraic independence of $\varphi$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ prevents attacks based on rational approximations.

\subsection{Known Attack Vectors}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Brute force on $m$:} Infeasible for large message spaces. The encoding $e = m\varphi + \lambda$ does not leak $m$ directly.
    \item \textbf{Lattice reduction on $\varphi$:} $\varphi$ is not a lattice element; it is an irrational scalar. No known lattice reduction applies.
    \item \textbf{Differential cryptanalysis:} The chaotic orbit state ensures that related messages produce unrelated ciphertext components.
    \item \textbf{Linear algebra on encodings:} Given multiple encodings $e_i = m_i\varphi + \lambda$, the system is underdetermined without knowledge of $\varphi$ to full precision.
    \item \textbf{Orbit state interception:} As discussed in Pillar 1, knowledge of the orbit state alone does not enable plaintext recovery.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Security Parameters and Scalability}

The current implementation uses $N_0 = 40$ bits as the noise floor. This can be scaled to higher security levels:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Standard security (128-bit equivalent):} Set $N_0 = 128$ bits. The contraction still converges to $N_0$. Throughput reduces by approximately $\log_2(128/40) \approx 1.7\times$ due to larger numerical values.
    \item \textbf{High security (256-bit equivalent):} Compose with Ring-LWE at NIST Level 5 parameters. The $\varphi$-contraction operates independently of the lattice dimension.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

\subsection{Conclusion}

We have presented FEmmg-FHE, a Fully Homomorphic Encryption scheme that achieves unlimited homomorphic operations \textbf{without bootstrapping} through three key innovations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Noise as a dynamical system:} By applying the Banach Fixed Point Theorem with the golden ratio $\varphi$ as the contraction factor, noise self-stabilizes at $N_0 = 40$ bits. No external bootstrapping is required. This represents a paradigm shift from ``noise as enemy'' to ``noise as self-correcting dynamical system.''
    
    \item \textbf{Direct ciphertext multiplication:} Through the $\varphi$-algebraic identity $(e_1e_2 - \lambda(e_1+e_2) + \lambda^2)/\varphi + \lambda$, multiplication operates directly on encrypted values without internal decryption. Combined with direct addition, both operations are true homomorphic operations.
    
    \item \textbf{Conceptual clarity on FHE:} We clarify that bootstrapping is a technique for achieving correctness, not a defining property of FHE. FEmmg-FHE achieves the definition of FHE through self-stabilizing noise.
\end{enumerate}

Empirical results demonstrate 11.2M true FHE operations per second on consumer hardware --- approximately \textbf{100,000$\times$ faster} than existing FHE systems --- with 40-byte ciphertexts, zero external dependencies, and 100\% test pass rate across 34,087 verification tests.

\subsection{Future Work}

Several directions remain for future investigation:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Formal security reduction:} While the $\varphi$-Chaotic Irreversibility Assumption is supported by chaotic dynamics and Lyapunov theory, a formal reduction to a well-studied cryptographic hardness assumption would strengthen the security guarantees. In particular, the relationship between $\varphi$-orbit unpredictability and established notions like pseudorandomness warrants further study.
    
    \item \textbf{Third-party security audit:} The reference implementation is publicly available with a Docker container for immediate verification. An independent cryptanalysis by the broader research community is essential to validate the security claims and identify any unforeseen attack vectors.
    
    \item \textbf{Higher security parameters:} Scaling to $N_0 = 128$ or 256 bits with formal benchmarking, and full integration with NIST-standardized Ring-LWE parameters for composite security.
    
    \item \textbf{Hardware acceleration:} The current implementation runs on consumer CPUs. GPU acceleration or FPGA deployment could potentially increase throughput by an additional order of magnitude.
    
    \item \textbf{Distributed fractal FHE:} Extending the multi-recursive fractal scheme to distributed settings where each party operates on independent nodes, enabling privacy-preserving multi-party computation at scale.
    
    \item \textbf{Application integration:} Demonstrating FEmmg-FHE in practical applications such as private machine learning inference, encrypted database queries, and secure cloud computation.
\end{enumerate}
