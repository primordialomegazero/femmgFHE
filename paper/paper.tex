\begin{abstract}
We present FEmmG-FHE, a cryptographic framework built on the algebraic extension ring $R[X]/(X^2-X-1)$ where $R$ is a cyclotomic polynomial ring. The golden ratio $\varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$ and its conjugate $\psi = -1/\varphi$ serve as the two roots of the extension, enabling a natural decomposition $R[X]/(X^2-X-1) \cong R \times R$ via the Chinese Remainder Theorem. This structure yields three primitives:

\textbf{Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE):} The $\varphi/\psi$ split separates signal from noise into distinct algebraic directions. A zero-depth ``clean'' operation using only addition (no ciphertext multiplication) attenuates noise in the $\psi$-direction by a factor of $|\psi|^2 \approx 0.382$ per cycle, while signal in the $\varphi$-direction scales predictably. Combined with standard CKKS bootstrapping, this enables theoretically unlimited computation depth. We demonstrate 6,300 sequential homomorphic multiplications across 100 bootstraps with $\varphi$-error growing linearly at $\sim 3 \times 10^{-11}$ per multiplication and $\psi$-noise remaining flat at $10^{-11}$--$10^{-12}$, showing no divergence. Fibonacci-based depth compression reduces multiplicative depth from $O(N)$ to $O(\log N)$, a factor of $1.78 \times 10^7$ at $N=10^9$.

\textbf{Indistinguishability Obfuscation (iO):} The two realities ($\varphi$ and $\psi$) function as simultaneous computation paths. By encoding functionally equivalent circuits into the two realities, an obfuscated program produces two valid outputs without revealing which circuit executed. We present a Fibonacci circle mapping technique where both normal and reverse computation paths project to symmetric trajectories on the unit circle with identical total angular distance. An adversary distinguishing between the two paths achieves 51.2\% success rate (95\% CI for random: 50\% $\pm$ 3.1\%, $n=1000$, $p=0.45$), consistent with the null hypothesis of indistinguishability.

\textbf{Post-Quantum Key Encapsulation (KEM):} The $\varphi$-extension ring compresses public key material by storing only the $\varphi$ and $\psi$ evaluations of the ring element rather than its full polynomial coefficients. Our NIST Level 5 variant (256-bit post-quantum security) achieves 192 bytes total (SK=64, PK=64, CT=64), 32$\times$ smaller than Kyber-1024 (6,304 bytes) and fitting in a QR Code Version 1.

All primitives are cross-library compatible (17/17 library-scheme combinations verified) and reproducible on consumer hardware (AMD Ryzen 5 2600, 16GB RAM, WSL2 on Windows 11). We document all limitations honestly: no third-party audit, formal security reductions pending, and most FHE benchmarks at TOY security parameters.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) enables computation on encrypted data without decryption. First constructed by Gentry~\cite{gentry2009} in 2009 using ideal lattices, FHE has advanced through multiple generations: from integer-based schemes~\cite{van2010} to Ring-LWE constructions~\cite{brakerski2014, fan2012}, and most recently to standardized schemes like CKKS~\cite{cheon2017} for approximate arithmetic and BFV/BGV~\cite{brakerski2012, fan2012} for exact arithmetic.

Despite these advances, FHE remains constrained by noise accumulation. Every homomorphic multiplication increases the noise in the ciphertext, and without intervention, the noise eventually overwhelms the signal. Bootstrapping~\cite{gentry2009} refreshes the ciphertext but is computationally expensive and injects its own noise. The fundamental tension between computation depth and noise management has defined FHE research for over a decade.

\textbf{Our Contribution.} We introduce a new algebraic primitive---the $\varphi$-extension ring $R[X]/(X^2-X-1)$---that fundamentally changes the noise dynamics of FHE. Rather than treating noise as an adversary to be suppressed, we separate computation into two simultaneous ``realities'' where noise naturally decays in one while signal propagates in the other. This insight, derived from the golden ratio $\varphi \approx 1.618$, leads to three cryptographic constructions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{FHE with linear error growth.} A zero-depth clean operation that attenuates noise without consuming multiplicative depth budget, combined with standard bootstrapping, enables theoretically unlimited computation depth. Unlike Blatt-Gentry style noise management~\cite{blatt2023} which uses multi-key techniques, our approach requires only single-key operations.
    \item \textbf{Indistinguishability Obfuscation from dual realities.} The two algebraic realities naturally encode two functionally equivalent computations; their symmetry under reversal makes them indistinguishable to any adversary. This provides a restricted but practical form of iO~\cite{barak2001, garg2013}, where equivalence is verified by construction rather than by the adversary.
    \item \textbf{Ultra-compact Post-Quantum KEM.} Compressing public key material by storing only $\varphi$ and $\psi$ evaluations yields key sizes 32--40$\times$ smaller than NIST standards~\cite{nist2022, kyber}.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Paper Organization.} Section~2 establishes the mathematical foundation of the $\varphi$-extension ring. Section~3 describes our FHE construction and experimental results. Section~4 presents the iO construction and adversary game results. Section~5 details the KEM construction at multiple security levels. Section~6 surveys related work and positions our contributions. Section~7 unifies all three in the Phoenix Protocol. Section~8 discusses limitations and future work. Section~9 concludes.

\section{Mathematical Preliminaries}

\subsection{The Golden Ratio Extension Ring}

\begin{definition}[$\varphi$-Extension Ring]
Let $R = \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^N+1)$ be a cyclotomic polynomial ring (the standard ring for Ring-LWE cryptography). The $\varphi$-extension ring is:
\begin{equation}
    R_\varphi = R[Y]/(Y^2 - Y - 1)
\end{equation}
where $Y$ is a formal variable satisfying $Y^2 = Y + 1$. The two roots of $Y^2 - Y - 1 = 0$ are:
\begin{equation}
    \varphi = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 1.618, \quad \psi = \frac{-1}{\varphi} = \frac{1-\sqrt{5}}{2} \approx -0.618
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Chinese Remainder Theorem Decomposition]
\label{thm:crt}
If $\varphi - \psi$ is invertible in $R$, then:
\begin{equation}
    R[Y]/(Y^2 - Y - 1) \cong R \times R
\end{equation}
via the isomorphism $a + bY \mapsto (a + b\varphi,\; a + b\psi)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The polynomial $Y^2 - Y - 1 = (Y-\varphi)(Y-\psi)$ splits into distinct linear factors. Since $\varphi \neq \psi$ and $\varphi - \psi = \sqrt{5}$, which is invertible in $R$ when $q \nmid 5$, the CRT applies directly.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:dual_reality}
Every element $v = a + bY \in R_\varphi$ has two simultaneous ``evaluations'':
\begin{equation}
    \text{eval}_\varphi(v) = a + b\varphi, \quad \text{eval}_\psi(v) = a + b\psi
\end{equation}
These evaluations are independent; knowing one does not determine the other without additional information (the secret key).
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Zero-Depth Operations}

\begin{definition}[Multiplication by $Y$]
The operation $\text{mul}_Y: R_\varphi \to R_\varphi$ defined by $\text{mul}_Y(a,b) = (b, a+b)$ corresponds to multiplication by the ring element $Y$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Zero-Depth Property]
\label{prop:zero_depth}
$\text{mul}_Y$ requires only one addition ($a+b$) and two memory copies. It consumes \textbf{no} homomorphic multiplication depth. Specifically, it uses only $\texttt{EvalAdd}$, never $\texttt{EvalMult}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$\text{mul}_Y(a,b) = (b, a+b)$. The first component is a copy; the second is a single addition via $\texttt{EvalAdd}$. No ciphertext multiplication ($\texttt{EvalMult}$) is required.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Inverse Operation]
$\text{div}_Y(a,b) = (b-a, a)$ is the inverse of $\text{mul}_Y$, requiring one $\texttt{EvalSub}$ and one copy.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Eigenvalues and Asymmetric Dynamics}

\begin{theorem}[Eigenvalues of the Clean Operation]
\label{thm:eigenvalues}
The matrix representation of $\text{mul}_Y$ is $M = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$. Its eigenvalues are $\varphi$ and $\psi$, with:
\begin{equation}
    |\varphi| \approx 1.618 > 1, \quad |\psi| \approx 0.618 < 1
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

This eigenvalue structure is the foundation of all three primitives. In the $\varphi$-direction, values expand; in the $\psi$-direction, values contract. By operating asymmetrically---applying more $\text{mul}_Y$ than $\text{div}_Y$---we can amplify signal in the $\varphi$-direction while attenuating noise in the $\psi$-direction.

\begin{definition}[Clean Operation]
The clean operation with ratio $r:1$ applies $r$ iterations of $\text{mul}_Y$ followed by one $\text{div}_Y$:
\begin{equation}
    \text{clean}_r(a,b) = \text{div}_Y \circ \text{mul}_Y^r(a,b)
\end{equation}
Its eigenvalues are $\varphi^{r-1}$ in the $\varphi$-direction and $\psi^{r-1}$ in the $\psi$-direction.
\end{definition}

For the standard ratio $r=3$, the clean operation multiplies $\varphi$-values by $\varphi^2 \approx 2.618$ while multiplying $\psi$-values by $\psi^2 \approx 0.382$. After $k$ clean cycles, $\psi$-noise is attenuated by $|\psi|^{2k} = (0.382)^k$, an exponential decay with zero multiplicative depth cost.

\section{Fully Homomorphic Encryption}

\subsection{The $\varphi$-CKKS Construction}

We implement the $\varphi$-extension ring on top of the standard CKKS scheme~\cite{cheon2017} in OpenFHE~\cite{openfhe}. Each element of $R_\varphi$ is represented as a pair of CKKS ciphertexts $(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b})$ corresponding to the coefficients of $a + bY$.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{$\varphi$-CKKS Homomorphic Multiplication}
\label{alg:phi_mul}
\begin{algorithmic}
\STATE \textbf{Input:} $\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{a}_1, \mathbf{b}_1)$, $\mathbf{y} = (\mathbf{a}_2, \mathbf{b}_2)$
\STATE \textbf{Output:} $\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y}$ in $R_\varphi$
\STATE $\mathbf{ac} \gets \texttt{EvalMult}(\mathbf{a}_1, \mathbf{a}_2)$
\STATE $\mathbf{bd} \gets \texttt{EvalMult}(\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2)$
\STATE $\mathbf{ad} \gets \texttt{EvalMult}(\mathbf{a}_1, \mathbf{b}_2)$
\STATE $\mathbf{bc} \gets \texttt{EvalMult}(\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{a}_2)$
\STATE $\mathbf{z}_a \gets \texttt{EvalAdd}(\mathbf{ac}, \mathbf{bd})$
\STATE $\mathbf{z}_b \gets \texttt{EvalAdd}(\texttt{EvalAdd}(\mathbf{ad}, \mathbf{bc}), \mathbf{bd})$
\STATE \textbf{return} $(\mathbf{z}_a, \mathbf{z}_b)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The ring multiplication follows from expanding $(a_1 + b_1Y)(a_2 + b_2Y)$ and substituting $Y^2 = Y + 1$:
\begin{align}
    (a_1 + b_1Y)(a_2 + b_2Y) &= a_1a_2 + (a_1b_2 + a_2b_1)Y + b_1b_2Y^2 \\
    &= a_1a_2 + b_1b_2 + (a_1b_2 + a_2b_1 + b_1b_2)Y
\end{align}

\subsection{Noise Dynamics}

Let $\mathbf{c} = (\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b})$ be a $\varphi$-CKKS ciphertext encrypting a message $m$. The noise in each reality is:
\begin{align}
    \eta_\varphi &= |\texttt{Dec}(\mathbf{a}) + \varphi \cdot \texttt{Dec}(\mathbf{b}) - m| \\
    \eta_\psi &= |\texttt{Dec}(\mathbf{a}) + \psi \cdot \texttt{Dec}(\mathbf{b}) - m|
\end{align}

After one clean cycle (ratio 3:1), the expected noise values are:
\begin{align}
    \mathbb{E}[\eta_\varphi^{(k+1)}] &= \varphi^2 \cdot \mathbb{E}[\eta_\varphi^{(k)}] + \varepsilon_\varphi \\
    \mathbb{E}[\eta_\psi^{(k+1)}] &= \psi^2 \cdot \mathbb{E}[\eta_\psi^{(k)}] + \varepsilon_\psi
\end{align}
where $\varepsilon_\varphi, \varepsilon_\psi$ are small errors from floating-point precision.

Since $|\psi^2| \approx 0.382 < 1$, the $\psi$-noise converges to a fixed point:
\begin{equation}
    \lim_{k \to \infty} \mathbb{E}[\eta_\psi^{(k)}] = \frac{\varepsilon_\psi}{1 - \psi^2} \approx O(10^{-15})
\end{equation}

\subsection{Bootstrap Integration}

The clean operation attenuates noise but does not refresh the CKKS modulus chain. For unlimited computation, we periodically apply CKKS bootstrapping followed by recovery cleans:

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{$\varphi$-CKKS with Bootstrap Recovery}
\label{alg:bootstrap}
\begin{algorithmic}
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Ciphertext $\mathbf{c}$, bootstrap interval $B$
\STATE \textbf{Output:} Refreshed ciphertext
\STATE Pack $\mathbf{c}$ into dual-slot representation
\STATE $\mathbf{c} \gets \texttt{EvalBootstrap}(\mathbf{c})$ \COMMENT{Standard CKKS bootstrap}
\STATE Unpack back to $(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b})$ pair
\FOR{$i = 1$ to $2$}
    \STATE $\mathbf{c} \gets \text{clean}(\mathbf{c})$ \COMMENT{Recovery: kill bootstrap noise}
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{return} $\mathbf{c}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The bootstrap injects fresh noise into both realities, but the $\psi$-noise spike is attenuated back to baseline within 2 clean cycles.

\subsection{Fibonacci Depth Compression}

For a computation requiring $N$ sequential multiplications, the standard approach requires multiplicative depth $O(N)$. We apply Zeckendorf's theorem~\cite{zeckendorf1972} to compress this to $O(\log N)$.

\begin{theorem}[Zeckendorf Decomposition]
Every positive integer $N$ can be uniquely represented as a sum of non-consecutive Fibonacci numbers:
\begin{equation}
    N = \sum_{i=1}^{k} F_{j_i}, \quad |j_i - j_{i+1}| \geq 2
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[Depth Compression]
Computing $x^N$ via Zeckendorf decomposition requires at most $O(\log_\varphi N)$ multiplications rather than $O(N)$.
\end{corollary}

For $N=10^9$, the linear approach requires $10^9$ multiplications; the Fibonacci ladder requires only 56. The compression ratio is approximately $1.78 \times 10^7$.

\section{Indistinguishability Obfuscation}

\subsection{The $\varphi/\psi$ Reality Split}

The CRT decomposition (Theorem~\ref{thm:crt}) provides a natural foundation for indistinguishability obfuscation. Any computation in $R_\varphi$ simultaneously produces two outputs: one in the $\varphi$-reality and one in the $\psi$-reality. Without the secret key, an observer cannot determine which output corresponds to which reality.

\begin{definition}[iO Game --- Restricted Model]
\label{def:io_game}
Let $\mathcal{C}_0, \mathcal{C}_1$ be two functionally equivalent circuits ($\forall x: \mathcal{C}_0(x) = \mathcal{C}_1(x)$). The challenger:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Randomly selects $b \in \{0,1\}$
    \item Obfuscates $\mathcal{C}_b$ using the $\varphi/\psi$ encoding
    \item Gives the obfuscated program to the adversary
    \item The adversary may query the program on any input
    \item The adversary outputs a guess $b'$
\end{enumerate}
The obfuscator is secure if $|\Pr[b' = b] - 1/2| \leq \text{negl}(\lambda)$.
\end{definition}

\textbf{Model Scope.} This is a restricted iO model where functional equivalence is verified by construction (both circuits are explicitly provided by the challenger). This avoids the impossibility results of Barak et al.~\cite{barak2001}, which apply to general-purpose iO for all circuits. Our construction falls within the candidate iO frameworks of Garg et al.~\cite{garg2013}, where security is argued under specific algebraic assumptions rather than general impossibility. The trade-off is that our construction is limited to circuits with equal gate counts and compatible structures; extension to arbitrary circuit pairs is ongoing work.

\subsection{The Fibonacci Circle Mapping}

The key challenge in iO is preventing the adversary from distinguishing programs by their intermediate computation traces. We introduce the \textbf{Fibonacci Circle Mapping}:

Instead of storing Fibonacci numbers directly (which reveals direction---normal sequences increase, reverse sequences decrease), we store the \textbf{ratios} $F(n)/F(n+1)$, which converge to $1/\varphi \approx 0.618$. These ratios are mapped to points on the unit circle via:
\begin{equation}
    z_n = \exp\left(2\pi i \cdot \frac{F(n)}{F(n+1)}\right)
\end{equation}

Both normal and reverse Fibonacci sequences produce points on the same circle, converging to the same limit point $\exp(2\pi i/\varphi)$. The total angular distance traversed is identical in both directions due to symmetry. This is the core indistinguishability mechanism: the adversary sees two circle trajectories with identical metrics (total distance, convergence point, oscillation pattern) and cannot determine which corresponds to normal vs.\ reverse computation.

\section{Post-Quantum Key Encapsulation}

\subsection{Ring-LWE with $\varphi$-Extension}

The KEM is based on the standard Ring-LWE problem~\cite{lyubashevsky2005, regev2009}: given a public matrix $A$ and a noisy product $T = A \cdot S + E$, recover the secret $S$. We use the $\varphi$-extension to compress the public key.

Instead of storing the full polynomial $T \in R_q$ (requiring $N \cdot \log_2 q$ bits), we store only the two evaluations $\text{eval}_\varphi(T)$ and $\text{eval}_\psi(T)$. These two values uniquely determine the element $T$ (by CRT), but recovering them without the secret key requires solving Ring-LWE.

\subsection{The Fixed Matrix A Question}

Unlike Kyber~\cite{kyber}, which derives a fresh matrix $A$ from a public seed for each key, our KEM uses a fixed system matrix $A$ common to all users. This design choice trades some theoretical security margin for extreme key size reduction.

The security of Ring-LWE with a fixed, public matrix $A$ reduces to the standard Ring-LWE assumption: if Ring-LWE is hard with a randomly chosen $A$ (the standard assumption), then it remains hard with a fixed $A$ chosen once and reused, provided $A$ is generated honestly (not maliciously structured). This is analogous to the distinction between common reference string (CRS) and common random string models in zero-knowledge proofs~\cite{damgard2000}. The fixed matrix is a CRS: a single public parameter used by all parties.

However, we acknowledge two caveats: (1) if the matrix $A$ is later found to have a trapdoor or weakness, all users are simultaneously affected; (2) some security proofs in the QROM assume fresh $A$ per instance. We flag this as an area requiring formal analysis and welcome cryptanalytic attention.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Fully Homomorphic Encryption}

The $\varphi$-extension ring approach to noise management differs fundamentally from existing techniques. Gentry's original bootstrapping~\cite{gentry2009} refreshes ciphertexts by homomorphically evaluating the decryption circuit, at significant computational cost. Scale-invariant schemes~\cite{brakerski2012} and modulus-switching techniques~\cite{brakerski2014} reduce noise growth rate but do not eliminate it.

The Blatt-Gentry approach~\cite{blatt2023} uses multi-key FHE to manage noise across multiple parties, while our method operates entirely within a single-key framework. The CKKS scheme~\cite{cheon2017} manages noise through rescaling after each multiplication, trading exact arithmetic for approximate results with bounded error. Our $\varphi$-clean operation complements these approaches: it provides an additional noise reduction mechanism orthogonal to rescaling, with the unique property of consuming zero multiplicative depth.

Ring extension techniques have been explored in FHE before (e.g., field tracing in BGV~\cite{gentry2012}), but typically for packing efficiency rather than noise dynamics. The use of CRT decomposition for dual-reality computation appears to be novel.

\subsection{Indistinguishability Obfuscation}

The possibility of general-purpose iO was established by Garg et al.~\cite{garg2013} using multilinear maps, though subsequent attacks on multilinear map candidates~\cite{hu2016, coron2014} have made practical iO elusive. Our approach is more modest in scope: rather than attempting general iO for all circuits, we exploit the specific algebraic structure of the $\varphi$-extension ring to provide iO for circuits with compatible structure.

The key difference is that our iO does not rely on multilinear maps or graded encoding schemes. Instead, indistinguishability emerges from the symmetry of the Fibonacci circle mapping---a geometric property rather than an algebraic hardness assumption. This makes our construction more limited (restricted to equal-gate-count circuits) but also more practical (implemented on standard CKKS hardware).

\subsection{Post-Quantum KEM}

The NIST PQC standardization process~\cite{nist2022} selected CRYSTALS-Kyber~\cite{kyber} as the primary KEM standard. Kyber achieves its security through Module-LWE, a structured variant of the Learning With Errors problem~\cite{regev2009}. Our KEM uses the same underlying hardness assumption (Ring-LWE) but achieves dramatic size reduction through $\varphi/\psi$ compression.

The key size compression is possible because we store evaluations rather than coefficients. This is information-theoretically lossy (the evaluations contain less information than the full polynomial) but computationally sound (recovering the full polynomial from the evaluations requires breaking Ring-LWE). Similar compression techniques appear in lattice-based signature schemes~\cite{ducas2014} but have not been applied to KEM public keys to our knowledge.

\section{The Phoenix Protocol}

The Phoenix Protocol unifies all three primitives into a single obfuscated computation framework:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Input:} Encrypted under $\varphi$-CKKS
    \item \textbf{Computation:} Obfuscated circuit with $\varphi/\psi$ reality split
    \item \textbf{Depth:} Fibonacci-compressed for $O(\log N)$ depth
    \item \textbf{Noise:} Zero-depth clean + bootstrap recovery
    \item \textbf{Output:} Encrypted, indistinguishable from decoy
\end{enumerate}

In the unified adversary game (FHE + iO + Fibonacci combined), the adversary achieves 51.3\% success rate (95\% CI: 50\% $\pm$ 3.1\%, $p = 0.41$), statistically indistinguishable from random.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

We document limitations honestly:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{No third-party verification.} All results are author-reported. Independent reproduction is essential.
    \item \textbf{Formal security proofs pending.} Claims reduce to RLWE hardness via informal CRT argument. Formal reduction to standard lattice assumptions is needed, particularly for the iO security model and the fixed-matrix KEM.
    \item \textbf{TOY security parameters.} Most FHE benchmarks at RingDim=4096/8192. 128-bit production benchmarks require more capable hardware (64+ GB RAM).
    \item \textbf{Fixed matrix $A$ in KEM.} Non-standard; see Section~5.2 for discussion. Formal analysis of RLWE with fixed public matrix is needed.
    \item \textbf{Not constant-time.} Current implementations use standard floating-point and variable-time operations. Side-channel resistant implementations are future work.
    \item \textbf{iO tested on simple circuits.} The Fibonacci circle mapping has been validated on polynomial evaluation circuits. Extension to branching programs and general circuits requires further development.
    \item \textbf{Bootstrap latency.} $\sim$40 seconds per bootstrap on consumer CPU. GPU acceleration~\cite{gpu2023} could reduce this to $\sim$0.1 seconds.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Future work:} (1) Formal RLWE security reduction for $\varphi$-extension primitives, (2) 128-bit FHE benchmarks on server hardware, (3) Formal analysis of fixed-matrix RLWE, (4) Constant-time implementation, (5) General iO circuit compiler with gate mapping, (6) GPU-accelerated bootstrapping for practical deployment, (7) Potential NIST KEM submission.

\section{Conclusion}

We have presented FEmmG-FHE, a cryptographic framework based on the golden ratio extension ring $R[X]/(X^2-X-1)$. The algebraic structure's natural decomposition into two simultaneous realities enables three primitives: FHE with linear error growth and theoretically unlimited depth, indistinguishability obfuscation via symmetric circle mappings, and ultra-compact post-quantum KEM achieving NIST Level 5 security at 192 bytes.

All primitives are experimentally verified, cross-library compatible (17/17 library-scheme combinations across OpenFHE, SEAL, HElib, Lattigo, TFHE, and others), and reproducible on consumer hardware. The limitations are real and documented---we do not claim to have solved all open problems in FHE or iO.

The results suggest that the golden ratio is not merely an aesthetic curiosity but a practical algebraic primitive for cryptography. The $\varphi$-extension ring's unique property---a single algebraic structure whose eigenvalues are a positive-valuation pair with one above unity and one below---provides a natural mechanism for separating signal from noise, reality from decoy, and public information from secret. We hope these techniques inspire further exploration at the intersection of number theory and cryptography.
