\begin{abstract}
We explore the algebraic extension ring $R[X]/(X^2-X-1)$---the golden ratio extension---as a primitive for lattice-based cryptography. The two roots $\varphi \approx 1.618$ and $\psi \approx -0.618$ create an asymmetry where one eigendirection expands while the other contracts. This structure enables several novel techniques that may reduce the computational cost of Fully Homomorphic Encryption.

We introduce \textbf{ratio encoding}, where values are represented as deviations from the $\psi$-attractor ($|\psi| \approx 0.618$) rather than as absolute magnitudes. Under Fibonacci-like state evolution (zero ciphertext multiplications), the ratio converges to $|\psi|$ and remains bounded, while the coefficient magnitude grows independently. This separation of ratio (value) from magnitude (noise carrier) suggests a new approach to managing plaintext approximation error in schemes like CKKS.

We also describe a \textbf{dual-reality architecture} where the CRT decomposition $R[Y]/(Y^2-Y-1) \cong R \times R$ enables state transfer between $\varphi$ and $\psi$ components via ring automorphisms. This provides a lightweight mechanism for modulus chain refresh without external bootstrapping.

Experimental results on consumer hardware (RingDim=4096, CKKS) demonstrate that basic homomorphic operations---addition, multiplication, and chains thereof---can be performed with algebraic bias corrections achieving $10^{-12}$ to $10^{-13}$ precision. We document all limitations honestly: no formal security reduction, toy parameters only, and the ratio encoding technique has been tested on a restricted set of operation patterns.

This work is exploratory. We present the $\varphi$-extension ring as a potentially useful algebraic primitive and invite further analysis, cryptanalytic review, and independent verification.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) enables computation on encrypted data. Since Gentry's construction~\cite{gentry2009}, successive generations of schemes~\cite{brakerski2012, brakerski2014, fan2012, cheon2017} have improved efficiency, but all face a common constraint: homomorphic operations accumulate noise in the ciphertext, eventually overwhelming the signal. The standard countermeasure---bootstrapping~\cite{gentry2009}---is computationally expensive.

This paper does \textbf{not} claim to solve the bootstrapping problem. Rather, we explore whether the algebraic structure of a particular ring extension---$R[X]/(X^2-X-1)$, the golden ratio extension---offers useful properties for FHE optimization. The core observation is simple: this ring has one expanding eigenvalue ($\varphi \approx 1.618$) and one contracting eigenvalue ($\psi \approx -0.618$, with $|\psi| < 1$). This asymmetry is unusual in cryptographic rings and may be exploitable.

\subsection{Our Contribution}

We propose and experimentally evaluate three techniques based on the $\varphi$-extension ring:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Ratio encoding:} Represent values as deviations from the $\psi$-attractor rather than as magnitudes. The ratio remains bounded under state evolution while the magnitude grows independently.
    \item \textbf{$\psi$-attractor dynamics:} Fibonacci-like state transitions (zero ciphertext multiplication cost) drive the state ratio toward $|\psi|$, providing a natural ``reset'' mechanism for the plaintext error component.
    \item \textbf{Dual-reality modulus refresh:} The CRT decomposition of the extension ring enables state transfer between $\varphi$ and $\psi$ components, refreshing the modulus chain without external bootstrapping.
\end{enumerate}

We emphasize that these are \textbf{techniques}, not a complete FHE scheme. They address specific computational bottlenecks---modulus refresh and plaintext error management---but do not replace the need for proper ciphertext noise management (bootstrapping or equivalent) in a fully homomorphic system.

\subsection{What This Paper Is NOT}

\begin{itemize}
    \item A claim to have ``solved'' FHE or eliminated the need for bootstrapping.
    \item A proposal for a production-ready cryptosystem.
    \item A peer-reviewed security analysis of the composite ring $R[X]/(X^N+1, X^2-X-1)$.
    \item A formal reduction to standard lattice assumptions.
\end{itemize}

\section{The $\varphi$-Extension Ring}

\subsection{Definition and Basic Properties}

\begin{definition}
Let $R = \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^N+1)$ be a cyclotomic polynomial ring (the standard ring for Ring-LWE-based FHE). The $\varphi$-extension ring is:
\begin{equation}
R_\varphi = R[Y]/(Y^2-Y-1)
\end{equation}
where the indeterminate $Y$ satisfies $Y^2 = Y+1$. The roots of this quadratic are $\varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2 \approx 1.618$ (the golden ratio) and $\psi = -1/\varphi \approx -0.618$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
The ring $R_\varphi$ is a free $R$-module of rank 2. Elements are represented as $a + bY$ with $a,b \in R$. The crucial property is that multiplication by $Y$ has one eigenvalue greater than 1 ($\varphi$) and one eigenvalue with absolute value less than 1 ($\psi$). In the following, we use $|\psi| = 0.6180339887498949$ to denote the absolute value.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[CRT Decomposition]
If $\varphi - \psi = \sqrt{5}$ is invertible in $R$, then:
\begin{equation}
R[Y]/(Y^2-Y-1) \cong R \times R
\end{equation}
via $a + bY \mapsto (a + b\varphi,\; a + b\psi)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}
The invertibility of $\sqrt{5}$ modulo $q$ depends on whether 5 is a quadratic residue mod $q$. This condition holds for approximately half of all primes $q$. When it fails, the CRT decomposition does not apply and the ring structure differs. This is a parameter constraint that must be considered in any practical instantiation.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Zero-Depth Ring Operations}

The operation $\text{mulY}(a,b) = (b, a+b)$ corresponds to multiplication by $Y$ in $R_\varphi$. Its matrix is:
\begin{equation}
M = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
with eigenvalues $\varphi$ and $\psi$. Critically, $\text{mulY}$ requires \textbf{zero ciphertext multiplications}---only a single homomorphic addition.

The inverse is $\text{mulY\_inv}(a,b) = (b-a,\; a)$, also zero ciphertext multiplications.

We also define two derived operations used in the DM-DGR cycle:

\begin{definition}[Forward Clean]
$\text{clean\_fwd}(a,b) = (a+b,\; a+2b)$. This applies $\text{mulY}$ twice in a fused operation, with eigenvalues $\varphi^2 \approx 2.618$ and $\psi^2 \approx 0.382$. Cost: 2 homomorphic additions.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Reverse Clean]
$\text{clean\_rev}(a,b) = (2a-b,\; -a+b)$. This applies $\text{mulY}$ followed by $\text{mulY\_inv}^2$, with the same eigenvalues but swapped eigenvectors. Cost: 2 homomorphic additions.
\end{definition}

The forward clean shrinks the $\psi$-component by $\psi^2 \approx 0.382$ while expanding the $\varphi$-component. Reverse clean does the opposite. This asymmetry is the basis for the cyclic reset strategy described in Section~4.

\subsection{Ratio Encoding}

Rather than encoding values in the magnitude of ciphertext coefficients, we encode them in the \textbf{ratio} of the two components:

\begin{definition}[Ratio Encoding]
A value $v \in \mathbb{R}$ is encoded as a state $(a,b) = (v + |\psi|,\; 1)$. The decoded value is:
\begin{equation}
\text{decode}(a,b) = \frac{a}{b} - |\psi|
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The constant $|\psi| \approx 0.618$ serves as a ``baseline'' ratio. Values are encoded as deviations from this baseline.

\subsection{The $\psi$-Attractor}

Under repeated application of $\text{mulY}$, the state ratio converges:

\begin{theorem}[$\psi$-Attractor]
For any initial state $(a_0, b_0)$ with $b_0 \neq 0$:
\begin{equation}
\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = |\psi| \quad \text{where } (a_n, b_n) = \text{mulY}^n(a_0, b_0)
\end{equation}
The convergence rate is $O(|\psi|^{2n}) \approx O(0.382^n)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}
This convergence is a \textbf{plaintext} property. It concerns the encoded values, not the cryptographic noise in the ciphertext. The RLWE noise grows according to standard Ring-LWE bounds and is unaffected by the ratio convergence. The utility of the $\psi$-attractor is for managing \textit{plaintext approximation error} in approximate FHE schemes like CKKS, not for managing \textit{cryptographic noise}.
\end{remark}

\section{Ratio-Based Homomorphic Operations}

\subsection{Operations on Ratio-Encoded States}

We define two primary operations that preserve the ratio encoding:

\textbf{Ratio Addition:}
\begin{equation}
\text{ratio\_add}((a_1,b_1), (a_2,b_2)) = (a_1b_2 + a_2b_1,\; b_1b_2)
\end{equation}

\textbf{Ratio Multiplication:}
\begin{equation}
\text{ratio\_mult}((a_1,b_1), (a_2,b_2)) = (a_1a_2,\; b_1b_2)
\end{equation}

Both operations introduce systematic algebraic biases in the decoded values. These biases are \textbf{deterministic} and can be corrected.

\subsection{Bias Correction Formulas}

We derive and experimentally verify the following corrections. All formulas use $|\psi| = 0.6180339887498949$.

\subsubsection{Pure Addition ($N$ consecutive additions)}

\begin{equation}
v_{\text{sum}} = \text{raw} - N \cdot |\psi|
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Pure Multiplication (two fresh operands)}

\begin{equation}
v_1 \cdot v_2 = \text{raw} - |\psi|(v_1 + v_2) + (2|\psi| - 1)
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Multiply-then-Add ($M$ multiplications, then 1 addition)}

\begin{equation}
\text{corrected} = \text{raw} - |\psi|\sum v_i + M(2|\psi|-1) - (M-1)|\psi|
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Add-then-Multiply ($N$ additions, then 1 multiplication)}

This operation chain introduces an additional offset from CKKS EvalMult rescaling in the \texttt{ratio\_add} internal operations:
\begin{equation}
\text{corrected} = \text{raw} - N\!\cdot\!|\psi|\!\cdot\!v_C - |\psi|\sum v_i - N\!\cdot\!|\psi|^2 + |\psi| - \delta(v_C)
\end{equation}
where $\delta(v_C) = |\psi|(|\psi| + v_C)$. For test parameter $v_C = 0.2$: $\delta = 0.5055728090$.

\begin{remark}
The offset $\delta(v_C)$ is derived from CKKS rescaling behavior and verified for $v_C = 0.2$. Generalization to arbitrary $v_C$ is derived but experimentally tested only for this value.
\end{remark}

\section{DM-DGR: A Cyclic Architecture with Modulus Refresh}

We combine the techniques into a cyclic execution model called DM-DGR (Dual Modulus -- Double Golden Ratio). Each epoch consists of five phases:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Forward computation} (3 $\text{clean\_fwd}$ operations): Drives $\psi$-noise toward the attractor while performing homomorphic computation.
    \item \textbf{EvalMult workload} (2 scalar multiplications): The actual encrypted computation.
    \item \textbf{Reverse reset} (1 $\text{clean\_rev}$ operation): Shrinks accumulated $\varphi$-error by approximately 62\%, at the cost of a temporary $\psi$-component spike.
    \item \textbf{Recovery} (1 $\text{clean\_fwd}$ operation): An additional forward clean that restores the $\psi$-component toward the attractor. The spike from the reverse clean self-heals within 2--3 forward cleans.
    \item \textbf{Ring swap} (when modulus levels drop below threshold): Transfers state to the idle reality via $\text{mulY}$ or $\text{mulY\_inv}$, obtaining fresh modulus levels.
\end{enumerate}

We tested this cycle for 50 epochs on consumer hardware. Key observations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Modulus levels oscillate between 25--39 without depleting. Ring swaps refresh the modulus chain before exhaustion.
    \item $\varphi$-value is controlled between 0.14--0.54, never diverging. Reverse cleans reset accumulated plaintext error.
    \item $\psi$-noise is stable at $10^{-5}$--$10^{-6}$, self-healing after each reverse clean spike via the recovery phase.
    \item 10 ring swaps executed successfully.
    \item Zero decrypt+re-encrypt operations needed.
\end{itemize}

\begin{remark}
The ring swap refreshes the \textbf{modulus chain} (enabling more operations before modulus exhaustion) but does \textbf{not} refresh cryptographic noise. This is a lightweight alternative to bootstrapping for modulus management only; a full FHE system would still require noise management via traditional bootstrapping or equivalent.
\end{remark}

\section{Other Applications}

\subsection{Compact Key Encapsulation (Preliminary)}

The two-dimensional structure of $R_\varphi$ enables compact representation of Ring-LWE keys. We sketch KEM variants achieving 80--192 bytes total size (compared to Kyber-512's 3200 bytes). However, these use fixed-matrix Ring-LWE---a \textbf{weaker assumption} than Kyber's Module-LWE with fresh matrices. These comparisons are illustrative only and do not imply equivalent security.

\subsection{Dual-Reality Program Encoding (Experimental)}

The two simultaneous evaluations of each $R_\varphi$ element suggest a form of program encoding where two algebraically equivalent circuit representations can be stored in the $\varphi$ and $\psi$ components. Over 1,000 trials, an adversary distinguishing which representation was used achieved 51.3\% accuracy---statistically indistinguishable from random guessing. However, since the circuits are functionally equivalent, their outputs are identical, and this property reduces to observing that both representations produce the same result. For context on the much stronger notion of indistinguishability obfuscation, see~\cite{garg2013}. We include this application for completeness but make no claims of cryptographic obfuscation.

\section{Limitations}

We emphasize the following limitations to prevent overinterpretation of our results:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{No formal security reduction.} We provide no reduction from the security of $R_\varphi$ to standard Ring-LWE or any other established assumption. The security of cryptosystems instantiated in this ring is unanalyzed.

    \item \textbf{The CRT decomposition is conditional.} It requires $\sqrt{5}$ to be invertible modulo $q$, which holds for approximately half of all primes. Parameters must be chosen carefully.

    \item \textbf{Toy parameters only.} All experiments use RingDim=4096, which is not cryptographically secure. Behavior at RingDim=32768 or 65536 is untested and may differ.

    \item \textbf{Ratio encoding manages plaintext error, not ciphertext noise.} The $\psi$-attractor convergence is a plaintext property. RLWE ciphertext noise grows independently and must be managed by other means.

    \item \textbf{Ring swap is modulus refresh, not noise refresh.} The ring swap extends the modulus chain but does not reduce cryptographic noise. It is not a replacement for Gentry-style bootstrapping.

    \item \textbf{FHE\_OFFSET verified for limited parameters.} The add-then-multiply correction $\delta(v_C) = |\psi|(|\psi|+v_C)$ is tested only for $v_C=0.2$.

    \item \textbf{Author-reported results.} All experiments conducted on a single consumer desktop. No third-party verification.

    \item \textbf{Not constant-time.} Vulnerable to timing side-channel analysis.

    \item \textbf{The dual-reality encoding is not iO.} It provides plausible deniability for functionally equivalent circuits, a weak property. No claim of indistinguishability obfuscation is made.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

We have explored the $\varphi$-extension ring $R[X]/(X^2-X-1)$ as a novel algebraic primitive for homomorphic encryption. Three techniques---ratio encoding, $\psi$-attractor dynamics, and dual-reality modulus refresh---show experimental promise on toy parameters.

The key open problems are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Security analysis.} A formal reduction (or attack) for the composite ring $R[X]/(X^N+1, X^2-X-1)$.
    \item \textbf{Scaling.} Experimental validation at RingDim=32768+ with production security parameters.
    \item \textbf{Noise growth characterization.} Rigorous analysis of how ratio encoding interacts with RLWE noise accumulation in deep circuits.
    \item \textbf{General bias correction.} Derivation and verification of the FHE\_OFFSET formula for arbitrary operation parameters.
\end{enumerate}

We make our code publicly available and invite independent verification, cryptanalysis, and further development. The $\varphi$-extension ring is mathematically interesting; whether it proves cryptographically useful remains to be determined.
